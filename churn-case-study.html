01 · Overview

The problem

A bank wanted to understand why customers were leaving and how to keep them. The raw data was a single unstructured CSV of 10,000 customers — demographics, financial indicators, and banking behavior — with no pipeline, no model, and no reporting.

My goal was to build the whole solution end-to-end: a repeatable data pipeline from ingestion to dashboard, a clear churn rate and risk segmentation, and actionable retention insights management could monitor over time. I owned every role on this project — business analyst, data engineer, data modeler, analyst, and BI developer.

Headline result: a 20.37% churn rate identified against a 15% target, with Germany, inactive members, and older customers pinpointed as the highest-risk segments — each mapped to a concrete retention recommendation.

03 · Business requirements (BRD)

Defining the questions first

Before touching data, I wrote a Business Requirement Document to anchor the work in real objectives.

Business objectives

  • Identify the characteristics of customers likely to churn
  • Quantify the churn rate and segment customers by risk level
  • Develop actionable insights for a retention strategy
  • Enable management to monitor churn KPIs through Power BI
  • Establish a repeatable pipeline from ingestion to reporting

Key business questions

  • What is the overall churn rate, and how does it break down by segment?
  • How do age, tenure, and balance influence churn likelihood?
  • Are inactive customers or non-cardholders more likely to churn?
  • Which geography or gender shows higher churn trends?
  • How do credit-score segments correlate with churn?

The dataset

10,000 rows of structured customer data — credit score, geography, gender, age, tenure, balance, products held, credit-card ownership, activity status, estimated salary, and the churn flag (Exited) — plus each customer's date of joining.

04 · Process

The pipeline, step by step

I structured the build as six sprints, managed in Jira, moving data through a layered Snowflake warehouse.

Source CSV→ Snowflake RAW→ Clean & Transform→ Snowflake CLEAN→ Star Schema (ANALYTICS)→ Power BI
1

Warehouse setup

Built the Snowflake database and warehouse with three schemas — RAW, CLEAN, ANALYTICS — and defined naming conventions.

2

Load raw data

Staged and loaded the CSV into RAW via COPY INTO, then validated row counts, headers, and data types.

3

Clean & transform

In Python: renamed columns, cast dates, mapped IDs to labels (geography, gender, churn status), dropped noise, and wrote the result to CLEAN.

4

Star-schema model

Designed a fact table (Fact_Customer_Churn) with six dimensions — Customer, Geography, Gender, Exit, Active, Credit Card — with FK constraints and an ER diagram.

5

EDA & SQL analysis

Univariate, bivariate, and correlation analysis in Python; core business questions answered with SQL in Snowflake.

6

Power BI dashboard

Connected Power BI to Snowflake, built DAX measures and enriched dimensions, and designed an executive dashboard.

05 · Key findings

What the data revealed

Geography

Germany churns 2× more

Germany's churn rate hit 32.4% — nearly double France (16.2%) and Spain (16.7%) — despite having the highest average credit score, pointing to non-financial causes.

Activity · corr −0.156

Inactive members churn ~3× more

The strongest behavioral signal. Active customers are significantly less likely to leave — engagement is the key retention lever.

Age · corr +0.285

Older customers leave more

Churn rises sharply with age — the 50s segment showed a 56% churn rate, versus under 8% for the under-30s.

Products

Product overload backfires

Customers holding 3–4 products churned the most (up to 100% at 4 products), suggesting complexity or product fatigue despite higher balances.

Gender

Women churn slightly more

Female churn (~25%) ran higher than male (~16%), most pronounced in Germany (~37.6%).

Trend

Improving since 2017

Churn peaked around 2017 (22.4%) and eased toward 19–20% by 2019 — but stayed above the 15% target every year.

Recommendations delivered

  • Re-engage inactive customers — the single biggest lever, with targeted campaigns for dormant high-value accounts
  • Investigate Germany — satisfaction surveys and competitor benchmarking, since churn there isn't credit-driven
  • Loyalty benefits for older, longer-tenure customers to counter age-linked attrition
  • Simplify multi-product relationships and cross-sell thoughtfully rather than overloading
